Fighting climate change — one tree, one forest, one child at a time

Madagascar is a frontline victim of global warming: deforestation, soil erosion, failing rains. SILVA is Education for Madagascar's direct response — building living forests that feed communities and absorb carbon for generations to come.

A dream made real

Beyond planting trees — building a living, edible forest

SILVA was born from a simple conviction: that restoring Madagascar's forests is not only possible, but urgent. The project plants edible tropical forests — ecosystems designed in five vertical layers, where each tree serves a purpose, feeds a family, and shelters the soil beneath it.

In 2021, 54 children and 21 educators made the journey to our site in Anjozorobe to plant the first layer of a living forest: eight tropical species chosen for their resilience, their nutritional value, and their ability to absorb carbon over decades.

Alongside this, in Morondava on the west coast, SILVA is restoring mangrove forests — among the most powerful carbon sinks on Earth, and a natural barrier against the cyclones that batter Madagascar's coastline each year.

The five layers of an edible forest

Layer 1 — Emergent · 20m+

The Canopy Giants

Acacia Ash Jacaranda

The tallest trees form the uppermost canopy — capturing maximum sunlight, breaking wind, and providing the primary carbon store of the forest. Their roots reach deep into the earth, holding the soil that rain once washed away.

Layer 2 — Sub-Canopy · 10–20m

The Food Providers

Murier Melia Eugenia Jambolana

Below the canopy giants, these trees produce fruit and timber — food security made permanent, harvest after harvest, without a single input of chemical fertiliser or irrigation.

Layer 3 — Shrub & Understory

The Soil Healers

Moringa Citrodora

Moringa — Madagascar's miracle tree — grows here, its leaves a superfood for children and a natural fertiliser for the soil around it. Citrodora fills the air and repels insects naturally. Every layer works for every other layer.

Layers 4 & 5 — Ground Cover & Roots

The Living Floor

Cover plants Root vegetables Fungi

The forest floor is never bare. Ground cover holds moisture, prevents erosion, and feeds the invisible world of fungi and microorganisms that make the whole system alive. Above ground, nothing is wasted. Below ground, everything is connected.

Morondava · West Coast

Mangroves — the ocean's forest

Mangrove ecosystems are among the most powerful carbon sinks on Earth — sequestering vast amounts of carbon within soil, leaves, branches, and roots. They are also Madagascar's most effective natural defence against the cyclones that devastate the coastline each year.

Working alongside our volunteers, SILVA has planted mangrove strips along the Morondava coast — small propagules that grow, slowly and surely, into a living wall between the sea and the communities behind it.

Why it matters

"A country that destroys its forests destroys itself.
A country that plants them — renews itself."

Madagascar has lost over 90% of its original forest cover. Every tree planted by a child in Anjozorobe is a small act of defiance against that trajectory — and a direct contribution to the global carbon budget.
