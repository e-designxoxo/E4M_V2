What is PERMADA?

Restoring desertified land into living, fertile soil — without chemicals

The Permaculture for Children project promotes a new vegetable gardening technique that restores originally desertified lands. By building a mound of dry branches, covered with leaves and dry vegetation, the soil transforms into rich, chemical-free humus within a single season.

Launched as a prototype in 2017 in Ambohibe, the project now spans a second site in Anjozorobe — four hectares of land experimenting with the same technique. The aim: food self-sufficiency for families, and ultimately the replication of these practices across communities.

Despite drought, floods, and hurricanes — productivity has only grown. Year after year, without a single bag of fertiliser.

The science behind it

A design philosophy, not a recipe

Permaculture is the art of creating sustainable human settlements by mirroring the patterns of natural ecosystems. It was coined by Bill Mollison and David Holmgren in the 1970s — and has proven especially powerful in island ecosystems like Madagascar's.

The project is guided by Permaculture Education Standards and its three ethical pillars.

How it works

From dead soil to fertile ground — four layers

No chemicals. No machines. Just biomass, patience, and an understanding of how the earth renews itself.

🫘 🌿 🥦 🫛 🌱
Humus · fertile topsoil
Rotting vegetation
Dry leaves
Dry branches — base mound
1

Build the mound

Stack dry branches and logs into a mound — this forms the structure and traps air pockets for microbial life.

2

Layer dry leaves

Cover the mound with fallen dry leaves — adding carbon, moisture retention, and protection from the sun.

3

Add dry vegetation

Grass clippings, crop residues, kitchen scraps — nitrogen-rich material that accelerates decomposition.

4

Humus forms naturally

Within a season, the layers become rich, dark humus — fertile topsoil that replaces expensive chemical fertilisers permanently.

Result: "A very fertile topsoil that will prevent the use of chemical fertilizers — for good."

What is Permaculture?

The six domains of permaculture design

Permaculture is a design philosophy that creates sustainable human settlements and agricultural systems by consciously mirroring the patterns and resilience found in natural ecosystems. The word fuses permanent + agriculture + culture — coined by Bill Mollison and David Holmgren in the 1970s.
Observe & Interact

Spend time understanding the land before acting — water flows, wildlife corridors, wind patterns, soil characteristics.

Catch & Store Energy

Harness sun, rain, and biomass at peak abundance — ponds, swales, compost, and food forests store nature's gifts for lean seasons.

Obtain a Yield

Design systems that meet human needs today without compromising tomorrow — food, medicine, shelter, and community wellbeing.

"The greatest change we need to make is from consumption to production, even if on a small scale, in our own gardens."

— Bill Mollison, co-founder of Permaculture
LES FONDATIONS

Three Core Ethics

Every permaculture design flows from three founding ethics — a moral compass that guides decisions from the smallest garden to the widest landscape.

01 Prendre soin de

Earth Care

Treat the Earth as a living system. Every action must restore soils, protect water, nurture biodiversity.

02 Prendre soin des

People Care

Provide access to food, water, shelter, education and meaningful community — with dignity and equity.

03 Partage équitable

Fair Share

Set limits on consumption and share surplus. Reinvest the abundance back into Earth care and people care.
