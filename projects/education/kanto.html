About the programme

Where creativity and strategy
build confident young leaders

Kanto means "culture" in Malagasy — and at KANTO, culture is taken in its broadest sense. Across eight weekly workshops, young people improve their languages, learn an instrument, play chess, dance, and create art. The programme runs every Saturday — and every day through the holidays — at the Ambohibe centre in Antananarivo.

Language opens the door to the world: French and Malagasy so every child becomes fluent in Madagascar's two official languages, plus English for a wider horizon. Music nurtures creativity and self-discipline. Chess sharpens reasoning and patience. Together, through practice, tournaments and rehearsals, KANTO grows a spirit of community, organisation, and quiet excellence.

🌍 An UNESCO Club partnership — uniting art, education and civic values.

Our approach

The children lead
their own learning

KANTO gives young people instruments, board games, art materials and tablets — then trusts them with the freedom to choose. Over time, each child takes charge of the workshops with only minimal guidance from a staff member.

That freedom of choice is the method, not an afterthought. When a child decides what to practise, motivation follows — and with it the self-discipline, organisation and spirit of excellence that KANTO exists to nurture. There is no competition in art; progress is simply made as one strives.

How it began

From old cans to a chorus of violins

In the beginning there were no instruments — only old cans the children drummed to keep the beat, a sound like the music of Brazil's favelas. Then came a piano. Later, four violins and two guitars.

Chess arrived the same way: three boards, donated. In a village with no electronic games or costly entertainment, the children of Ambohibe learned to wage patient, peaceful battles on the board — and their humble club, full of potential, held its first tournament.

Today every birthday at KANTO is celebrated in song, with two guitars and three different voices. That is the heart of it: not what the children are given, but what they build with it.
